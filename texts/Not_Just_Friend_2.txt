After negotiating that, I went and sat at the desk, feeling excited about finding an art store and ignoring the other reason I was there. I found a small independent shop in Aurora, which wasn’t too far. I just hoped they’d have something that wasn’t out of my mom’s price range. However, I did have some money saved up that I’d be willing to spend on getting set up. Watching all the arty TikToks had me wanting to try a few things.
I clicked around a bit, looking at what sorts of pencils I should get and stuff, but the store closed at five on Sundays and we’d have to leave by four to have time to look around. I reluctantly navigated to my inbox. Mom had forwarded the email, but I’d never looked at it. I didn’t really look at email at all usually, so it had been easy to avoid.
I taped my fingers on the laptop case for a while before finally clicking on the email with the subject line Fw: Reaching out about Jenica.
Even the subject line annoyed me. My full name was Jenica Estes Nichols. The last name was easy enough, but I had good ol’ Dad to thank for the rest of that name. Dad had wanted a J name to go with his and he chose Jenica, which meant God is gracious. This was both annoying for an atheist like me and often tripped teachers up on the first day of school, meaning I’d have to raise my hand and say Jen in front of the whole class. It was embarrassing. Estes was because of Estes Park in the Rocky Mountain National Park.
My dad had wanted to live in Colorado because of the mountains. He had dreams of hiking in the mountains, bagging fourteeners. He and my mom had married right after high school and moved to Colorado. He’d been disappointed to settle in Prairie View, which was a good drive away from the mountains, but Mom had gotten a job at the airport and it was where they could afford to live in striking distance of the airport. On her days off, they’d drive to the mountains and hike, which more or less worked until Mom got pregnant with me. Or more accurately, until Mom was very, very pregnant with me and couldn’t hike anymore. Dad, who’d not gotten a job, started going more and more by himself, camping overnight on longer and longer trips. When I was a few months old, Mom gave him an ultimatum. Either he found a job and stayed home at least twenty nights a month or she was divorcing him. He chose the mountains.
This was the first either of us had heard from him since, and yet my name was all about him from beginning to end. It was gross.
